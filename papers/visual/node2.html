Background

A speech signal (Figure 1(a)) is a pressure wave that travels from a speaker to the listener[5]. This signal essentially consists of variation in pressure as a function of time. The amplitude variations of such a signal correspond to deviations from atmospheric pressure caused by the traveling waves. The main organs of the human body responsible for producing speech are the lungs, larynx, pharynx, nose and various parts of the mouth. Muscular force to expel air from the lungs provides the source of energy. The air flow is modulated in various ways to produce components of acoustic power in the audio frequency range. The properties of the resultant sound are modified by the rest of the vocal organs to produce speech.

The process of acoustic resonance is of prime importance in determining the properties of speech sounds. The vocal tract, that starts at the larynx and extends up through the pharynx and mouth to the lips, is the principal resonant structure. For some sounds the nose is also coupled in to make a more complicated resonant system. The frequencies of the resonance and the way they change with time are crucial in determining what is being said. The main resonance modes of the vocal tract are known as formants, and by convention they are numbered from the low-frequency end. They are usually referred to as F1, F2, F3, etc. In general F1 and F2 (normally in the range 250 Hz to 3 KHz) are the most significant in determining the phonetic properties of speech sounds. frequencies are often considered as the elements of the feature vector, which characterizes the speech signal.

A short-time Fourier transform (STFT) of a speech signal gives the spectral representation of a speech signal. The spectrum gives a picture of the distribution of frequency and amplitude at a moment in time. The formants are best visualized in the spectral domain as spectral peaks. Fourier analysis done over successive overlapping blocks of the speech signal generates a three-dimensional plot called the spectrogram (see Figure 1(b)). The x-axis of the spectrogram represents time, the y-axis represents the spectral content (frequency), and the z-axis representing the spectral energy forming the three dimensions.

A spectrogram of the speech signal (Fig. 1(a)) is shown in Fig. 1(b). In essence, it is a projection of the three-dimensional Fourier plot onto the image plane. The spectrogram shows intensity variations, ranging from dark to shades of gray colour, which reflect the energies of the different frequencies occurring in the speech signal. The dark areas correspond to higher spectral energies are the formants, while the lighter areas denote lower spectral energy. The formants show a regular connected band like structure, because the human speech articulatory system is limited by certain physical constraints, which restrict the way the formants change, both over time and in frequency [8]. For this reason, we believe that the spectrogram has visual cues which can be used effectively to extract and track formants.

In this paper, we take the spectrogram of the speech signal, represented as a 8-bit gray scale image, as the input to our formant extraction and tracking technique.
